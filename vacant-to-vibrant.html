Margaret Fabrizio: A Kaleidoscopic Life

95 Years of Curiosity, From Carnegie Hall to the Siddi Forest.

Margaret Fabrizio does not live in the past; she creates the future. At 95, Margaret is a San Francisco icon whose creative output is as prolific as it is unpredictable. A world-renowned harpsichordist who taught at Stanford for 25 years, she has spent the last two decades reinventing herself as a master of textile art, an absurdist filmmaker, and a digital storyteller.

This pop-up retrospective will be a living 'collage' of a life dedicated to the 'what if?'—inviting you into a world where 17th-century Baroque music meets 21st-century YouTube performance, and where every scrap of fabric has a story to tell.

The Stitch (Quilts & Kawandi)

Margaret's quilts are a masterclass in improvisation. While her early works followed prize-winning traditional patterns, her practice was transformed in 2011 after a journey to India to study with the Siddi women. Learning the Kawandi technique—a method of hand-stitching from the outside-in—Margaret embraced a 'slow-stitch' philosophy. Her current collection of 50+ works features vibrant Indian saris, recycled kimonos, and 'leftover' scraps, with many pieces finished with the signature phula (flower) corners.

The Sound (Harpsichord & Performance)

Long before she picked up a needle, Margaret was a pioneer of the harpsichord. As the first artist to perform Bach's Art of Fugue on the harpsichord in America, her musical career spanned from the rigid prestige of Carnegie Hall to the experimental energy of 1970s San Francisco. Whether performing on a synthesizer atop Twin Peaks or collaborating with the Grateful Dead, Margaret's music has always been about pushing boundaries and finding the rhythm in the unexpected.

The Reveal (Portrait Masks & Collage)

For Margaret, a mask is not a way to hide—it is a way to reveal. Her 'Portrait Masks' are built directly onto the subject's face and then meticulously collaged with imagery that reflects their inner life. This body of work gained global recognition when one of her masks—a haunting, multi-eyed creation—was featured on the cover of the Grateful Dead's 1987 album, In the Dark. Her collages and handmade art books continue this theme, weaving together fragments of memory into a cohesive whole.

The Screen (Film & The Digital Archive)

An 'early YouTuber' in every sense, Margaret has been documenting her creative experiments digitally since 2004. Her channel is a treasure trove of the avant-garde, featuring everything from travelogues in Myanmar to 'absurdist performative skits.' With titles like Samurai Trapped by Macaroons, these films showcase Margaret's razor-sharp wit and her refusal to be defined by her age. Her films don't just document her art; they are her art.

The Documentary

The Kaleidoscopic project is also the site of an active film production. We are currently documenting Margaret's process as she curates this retrospective and engages with the San Francisco community. This space serves as both a gallery and a film set—a place where the past 95 years of work meet the lens of the future.
